Why buyers are choosing new builds

New construction has quietly become one of the best deals in the Utah market. Builders carry inventory they need to move, which means they're offering incentives like rate buydowns, closing cost credits, and free upgrades that regular sellers simply can't match.

But buying new is a different game than buying resale. The contract is written by the builder. The sales agent in the model home works for the builder. And the price on the sign is rarely the whole story. This guide walks you through the game so you can play it well.

Good to know: The builder's on-site agent represents the builder, not you. Bringing your own agent costs you nothing (the builder pays the commission), and it means someone at the table is negotiating for your side.

The leverage

Builder incentives: the real negotiation

Builders hate cutting the sticker price. It hurts the appraised values of every other home in the neighborhood. So instead, they negotiate with incentives. Common ones right now:

  • Interest rate buydowns: the builder pays to lower your mortgage rate, sometimes for the first few years, sometimes for the life of the loan. This can be worth tens of thousands of dollars.
  • Closing cost credits: the builder covers part or all of your closing costs, often tied to using their preferred lender.
  • Free or discounted upgrades: appliance packages, flooring, blinds, landscaping, or a finished basement thrown in.
  • Price reductions on standing inventory: homes that are already built and sitting are where builders get most flexible.
Timing tip: Builders have quarterly and year-end sales goals. The last weeks of a quarter, and especially December, are when incentives get most generous on move-in-ready homes.
What's available

Inventory and timelines: why they decide everything

Every new construction home falls into one of three buckets, and each comes with its own timeline. Understanding that connection is the single biggest thing that will sharpen your decision making, because your move-in date narrows your real options before you ever look at a floor plan.

  • Dirt (to-be-built): You pick the lot and the plan, and the builder builds it for you. Most choice, longest wait, typically 6 to 12 months.
  • Spec homes: Already under construction. You may still get to choose some finishes depending on the stage. Usually 1 to 4 months out.
  • Move-in ready: Finished and sitting. Least customization, fastest close, and the biggest incentives. This is the inventory builders most need to move.

Here's how the correlation plays out in real life. If your lease ends in 60 days, dirt is off the table no matter how much you love the floor plan, and move-in ready inventory is your market. If you have 9 to 12 months of runway, dirt gives you maximum choice and time to plan. Spec homes are the middle path: some choice, a manageable wait, and often solid incentives. Buyers who skip this step fall in love with homes they can't actually buy on their timeline. Buyers who start here shop only what fits, negotiate from a position of clarity, and never feel rushed into the wrong home.

What the build itself looks like

For a to-be-built home, here's roughly what to expect:

  • Weeks 1 to 2: Contract and lot selection. You sign the builder's purchase agreement and put down an earnest deposit, often larger than resale, sometimes $5K to $20K or more.
  • Weeks 2 to 6: Design center. You select finishes and upgrades. This is where budgets quietly balloon. More on that below.
  • Months 2 to 8: Construction. Foundation, framing, mechanicals, drywall, finishes. Weather and supply chains can shift this.
  • Final month: Walkthroughs. A pre-drywall walk if offered (ask for it), then a final "blue tape" walkthrough where you flag defects.
  • Closing. Standard closing process, then keys.
Protect yourself: Hire your own independent home inspector, even on a brand-new home. Yes, really. New homes fail inspections all the time. Builders move fast and subcontractors make mistakes. A few hundred dollars here can save you thousands.

The fine print

What's included vs. what's an upgrade

The gorgeous model home you tour is almost never the base home. Models are loaded with upgrades, often $50K to $150K worth. The base price gets you the "included features" sheet, and everything else costs extra.

Commonly not included in base pricing:

  • Refrigerator, washer, and dryer
  • Blinds and window coverings
  • Backyard landscaping (and sometimes front)
  • Upgraded flooring, countertops, and cabinet hardware you saw in the model

Smart upgrade strategy: Spend builder money on things that are expensive to change later, like structural options, extra windows, taller ceilings, and rough-in plumbing for a future basement bathroom. Skip things you can do cheaper after closing, like light fixtures, blinds, and landscaping.

The money

Financing a new build

Most builders have a preferred lender (national builders often own theirs outright) and tie their best incentives to using them. That's usually a fine deal, but not automatically.

  • Always get a second quote. Take the preferred lender's offer to an outside lender and compare the full picture: rate, fees, and the incentive combined.
  • Ask about rate locks. On an 8-month build, rates can move a lot. Ask whether you can lock, for how long, and what an extension costs. Some lenders offer a "lock and float down," where you're protected if rates rise and you benefit if they fall.
  • Budget past the mortgage. New neighborhoods often carry HOA fees and, in some Utah developments, special assessments or higher property tax as the area builds out. Get the numbers in writing.

Proceed with caution

Red flags to watch for

Pressure to sign same-day. "This incentive expires tonight" is a sales tactic. Legitimate incentives are almost always available tomorrow too.
No pre-drywall walkthrough allowed. If a builder won't let you (or your inspector) see the home before the walls close up, ask why.
Vague or verbal promises. If it's not written into the contract or an addendum, it doesn't exist. "We'll take care of that" means nothing at closing.
Earnest money that isn't clearly refundable, or isn't clear when. Understand exactly what happens to your deposit if financing falls through or the build is delayed.
A one-sided contract with no review period. Builder contracts are written by builder attorneys. Have someone on your side read it before you sign.
Bring this list

Questions to ask every builder

  • Rule number one before any of these: do not tour the model home without an agent representing you.
  • What incentives are you currently offering, and do they require your preferred lender?
  • What exactly is included in the base price? Can I get the included features sheet?
  • How much did the upgrades in this model home cost, all-in?
  • What's your average delay beyond the estimated completion date?
  • Can I do a pre-drywall walkthrough with my own inspector?
  • What does your warranty cover at 1 year, 2 years, and 10 years?
  • What are the HOA fees, and what do they cover?
  • Under what conditions is my earnest money refundable?
  • Are there lot premiums, and which lots carry them?
  • What happens to my rate lock if construction runs long?
